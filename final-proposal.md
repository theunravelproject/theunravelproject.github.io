## Introduction (ANANYA)

There is no doubt that the Digital Age has brought about undeniable progression in how we engage with history, politics, and philosophy. There has been an expansion in communication, access to knowledge, and newfound realms of reality that have arisen rapidly. Yet right alongside, we find ourselves at the center of information overload, lack of clarity, and general confusion about how to navigate the spaces that we now depend on. Especially in a time of great political strife, the precipice of war, and the country-wide loss of presence, most of the public, especially the working-age population, seek to clarify and navigate these overwhelming spheres of knowledge. Now mediums of education have been one of the most expansive results of the digital sphere, and with them, comes great responsibility. Most of the American population has noticed that the majority of our news is now most readily accessed through social media, even the government communicates with Americans through “X” more than cable-televised announcements, and for the majority of schools, a computer is the chief textbook. 

We propose a public broadcasting network, called UNRAVEL, to host an array of accessible scholarly knowledge in the form of video essays, a popular form of intimate scholarship that provides all of the qualities of a written essay, with the advantage of direct engagement with the authors. Accompanying the new relational element, we provide an array of interactive visualizations of our knowledge through digital collections and other cutting-edge data graphics. We seek to minimize ambiguity in the web of knowledge through direct access to discussion, transparency, and acknowledgement. 

## Episode 1: PSYOPtics (SYD)

Our project, we will be creating a Public Broadcasting Network which will employ researched video essays and collections, to empower historical, philosophical, and political engagement, and discussions of modern America. Hosted on a static site, available through more than just 5G internet, UNRAVEL’s first episode, and the introduction to our network, is called “PSYOPtics,” which centers on a video essay and digital collection on the developing relationship between domestic Psychological Operations in the United States and the American public imagination. This episode will use the history of COINTELPRO, a domestic Cold War psychological program formed by the FBI to target American political extremist organizations, to better understand how political life and American reality at large have been impacted by politically motivated psychological warfare. The Counter-Intelligence Program (COINTELPRO) existed covertly from 1956 and 1971 when it was discovered by a congressional committee. The program employed many psychological tactics across the United States to dismantle organizations and maintain the status quo of American, capitalist, political thought. 

We will use COINTELPRO as a case study and comparative lens for the effects of domestic psychological operations on American civilians. This historical moment will be analyzed beside post-modern notions of PSYOP, which are thought to be disseminated through digital media sites to reassert a dominant capitalistic understanding of reality. The episode will define these two moments of PSYOP and American imagination as “Cold War PSYOP” and “Digital Age PSYOP,” with our digital-age research covering roughly 2010 to the present. These moments in time will be assessed for their impact on reality, as well as their political, philosophical, and historical implications, to answer the following questions:

- How have domestic CIA Psychological Operations impacted not just the shared memory of American citizens, but American reality at large?
- How has PSYOP evolved through time following the digital revolution? How has this evolution worked to enforce or reinforce Cold War narratives? 

The use of comparative analysis between history and the present will ground our questions, and the episode at large, in the broader goals of our Public Broadcasting Network.  After engaging with PSYOPtics, we hope for our viewers to be able to have answers to our questions, critically engage with the current public imagination, and continue the discussion on the progression of PSYOP and its effects. 

## Website Mission (ANANYA)

The public broadcasting network will be a flexible, yet polished static site that allows for viewers to engage with and discuss the topics at hand with ample access to the data in discussion within each episode. The idea is that each episode may have unique digital medium attached to it; in the case of “PSYOPtics,” a curated digital collection of artifacts and documents about various domestic psychological operations, some being directly released government documents entailing operations, others including civilian media related to said operations or the public imagination which has been shaped by them, i.e. newspaper articles, television clips, propagandized media, etc.

Our network is shaped by a series of intentions, entailed by a Social Manifesto, created and located in its own tab of the network website, available for all users. The manifesto is made up of five principles that guide all media deployed on the site. 
Here are the tenets:
1. Strive for Transparency & Accessibility

2. Continually look for the gaps & silences in the scholarship we share

3. Welcome discourse between the user and the collection

4. Promote positionality in knowledge production 

The intention behind creating a manifesto is to emphasize our goals in creating an ethical post-modern digital humanities project. We would like to make our intentions transparent to our viewers and allow the audience autonomy to make an informed decision about the knowledge that they are engaging with. Poetics and narrative play a tremendous role in the production of historical knowledge, and acknowledging the nature of truly nuanced scholarship will be invaluable to expanding the public imagination. As Hayden White encompasses in “The Historical Text as Literary Artifact” in Geoffrey Roberts ed., The History and Narrative Reader, “We may only know the actual through contrast with, or likening to, the imaginable.”

The direct intention of UNRAVEL is to provide a space for people to engage with research and scholarship in a way that allows for more intimate interaction with the material. We aim for clarity, support, and innovation for each of our viewers. After engaging with the network, we hope for viewers to come away feeling like they not only have a great understanding of the topic and access to resources, but to feel inclined to discussion, to breakthroughs, to widen the web that each topic has opened. We intend for our viewers to gain a better understanding of modern topics while understanding their place in the topic, our place in facilitating knowledge, and the topic’s place in their own lives. Understanding that bias runs rampant and being able to notice it, work with it, and minimize are essential to UNRAVEL. We hope for the network to be one of many spaces for expansive engagement, a model for what accessible, interesting, and reliable knowledge can look like.

## Digital Collection (SYD)
Our project intends to include a digital collection component, which will be used as an interactive, curated bibliography for our video essay. Unlike a traditional bibliography, which often does not involve strict copyright rules, our use of primary documents in a collection space requires us to follow the copyright laws of the archives we are using. The government documents necessary for our collection will come from the FBI ‘Vault’ archive, and are free from copyright policy under the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA), which makes these declassified documents public domain. However, our use of civilian documents, like newspapers, will be sourced from Ancestry.com’s Newspaper archive, which does require the permission of the site to be used in a collection. The site writes, “The design, content, and graphics of those sites and all other elements created by our group companies or us (as applicable), or by third parties as work for hire, or where the copyright has been assigned to us or one of our subsidiaries, are subject to copyright protection with all rights reserved. To obtain permission to use any of these copyrighted elements, which permission may be granted or refused in our sole discretion, please write to copyright@ancestry.com with a full description of the element that you desire to use and the purpose, place, and extent of use intended. We may permit or deny any requests at our sole discretion.” In the case this request is denied, we are still able to cite and reference them in our collection, but may not be able to include the newspaper scan on our site. 

The last document format that will make up our digital collection is the modern social media post, which poses a new copyright dilemma that is more ethical than legal. Researchers collecting social media posts are not legally required to ask for consent for the users' intellectual property, as these sites, like X or Instagram, are in the public domain. This may pose a risk to the user who will have more eyes on them than expected, especially when the posts involve politically charged language. Alexis Shore Ingber, author of “Screenshots in Human Communication Research: Protecting Privacy in Light of Law, Ethics, and User Expectation,” writes, “Indeed, US law contains less emphasis on digital privacy compared to the United Kingdom and European Union (EU) despite being a major global hub for technology innovation (Almeida et al., 2022). Thus, it is significant to understand how a country whose privacy rights are fragmented may apply digital ethics standards and respond to user expectations of privacy across platforms. Rather than treating the US legal context as a territorial limitation, it can be viewed as a lens for understanding how researchers can still prioritize digital privacy when methodological advancement outpaces the law.” To protect users, we will anonymize the usernames of any post we collect, unless the user is a public figure or has a verified account. Although these techniques are not fail-proof, they will mitigate potential harm to users who have no consented to the use of their intellectual property.    
  
As for how we frame the information in our collection, including the aesthetic of our collection, it will be important to find examples of other collections. Looking at other digital history projects that center on a collection database, we better understand how we want to construct our own collection to meet our needs. Examining two unique digital collections projects, one which is a complete collection made through GitHub’s Collection Builder tool, and another which is a curated collection, we can see the pro’s and con’s to both forms of collections. Overall, our collection will employ the positives of both sites in order to develop a contextualized and highly detailed source of knowledge.   
	
The digital collection, “Lynching Postcards ​1908,” was created by the site Truth in Photography to tell the history of the popular trend of African American lynching postcards, which were collected and mailed in the 18th century United States. This collection project contextualing digitized version of original lynching postcards, placing them in the story of American history. The site writes, “While lynching photographs are inherently racist, the ethics of exhibiting them are complicated. Should they be seen in public, or should they be shrouded? The truth of the images is undeniable. Today, these photo postcards are a stark reminder of the brutality to which African Americans were and are subjected.” The collection is highly curated, with only a few images selected to convey the story of black lynching postcards. Though these digital objects are not fully described, as the only direct information about them is the archive from which they came. This collection is deeply powerful, not only because of the history being told, but also the contextualization that surrounds each digitized object. We hope to employ this method of contextualization and curation while constructing our own digital collection. 

The second site, titled “Status and the Sacred: Books of Hours in the Medieval World,” is a GitHub Collection Builder product, which provides an expansive amount of information about each digital object. This information includes the name, date, location, creator (if known), source, and image type. Allowing researchers to know vital details about each medieval object and its digital form. However, this information is not contextualized, leaving it without a place in the broader history of Western Europe. With the collection about us page writing, “This site is intended to serve as a resource for information about books of hours and certain information about Medieval Western European life associated with them. It is primarily designed to serve as a repository for scans of a book of hours in the collection of the Lilly Library at Indiana University Bloomington.” Overall, the amount of information provided for each digital object will be essential to the construction of our own collection.

## Literature Review

### *Cold War: 1947-1991* (SYD)
The history of American Intelligence as a branch of study has evolved through several decades. Around the 1970s, the number of scholars researching and referencing American Intelligence skyrocketed following the release of some intelligence records, as well as the disarray of Nixon scandals like Watergate (Ferris, 87). Richard Bett’s 1975 piece Soldiers, Statesmen, and Cold War crisis can be described as the ideal of the field during the time. American Intelligence studies often incorporated political science and history methods to analyze this history. Ferris describes in “Coming in from the Cold War” that these techniques would come specifically from existing paradigms in military history and strategic studies (Ferris 89). Through the evolution of the field, a major understanding of this work emerged. This was the understanding that the records would always be strategically released, weak, full of gaps, and thus would be the fate of the writing. Ferris writes, “The existing body of work is polticized, written to shape a private and /or public debate about the intelligence community. There is nothing wrong with that fact– as long as it is recognized” ( Ferris 92) This is an understanding of the field of research that will be important to be understand throughout our literature review, as well as in the development of the video essay and digital collection. This aspect of the American intelligence historiography is one we hope to address, adding to the field through our site’s transparency.      

For the Cold War section of our episode “PSYOPtics,” we will use both primary and secondary resources to research and discuss COINTELPRO and Cold War psychological operations more broadly. ### *The Age of the Internet: 2010-Present* (ANANYA)

“Automated Text Analysis for Intelligence Purposes: A Psychological Operations Case Study,” a chapter of Open Source Intelligence and Cyber Crime by Bryneilsson et al., examines how modern data-driven methods of analysis alter and progress the process of analysis within “intelligence tasks,” or more specifically, Psychological Operations. We intend to use this source to examine the development of psychological operations into the age of the internet. With relatively newfound accessibility to text analysis tools for data mining, the vast increase in data size, assisted by data mining opens doors for previously unheard of capabilities for fast tracking and disguising intelligence operations. Although this case study addresses foreign operations, it heavily informs the progression of psychological operations domestically as well. The book is housed on Springer Nature Link, a research publishing platform for academic scholarship worldwide.

“On influence operations: brainpower as a weapon of choice” by G.L. Lamborn, a retired CIA agent and Army reserve officer, is an undoubtedly interesting take on the state of modern PSYOPs. Although Lamborn has written a credibly sourced article, his position as a former CIA agent is unlike the variety of dominant views. He depicts a complex disappointment with the state of Psychological operations, sharing his opinions about the necessity to rebuild the former "brainpower" that the US had possessed during influence operations. He calls for an increase in PSYOP funding, a reemphasis on its integration into war planning, and reengaged to the frontlines. We plan to use this source in our comparative analysis to address the modern perspectives on Psychological Operations from a non-civillian standpoint as it has developed in the digital age, Lamborn’s take being especially interesting as a pro-PSYOP position.
