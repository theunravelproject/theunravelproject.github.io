## *Website Mission* (ANANYA)

The public broadcasting network will be a flexible, yet polished static site that allows for viewers to engage with and discuss the topics at hand with ample access to the data in discussion within each episode. The idea is that each episode may have unique digital medium attached to it; in the case of “PSYOPtics,” a curated digital collection of artifacts and documents about various domestic psychological operations, some being directly released government documents entailing operations, others including civilian media related to said operations or the public imagination which has been shaped by them, i.e. newspaper articles, television clips, propagandized media, etc.

Our network is shaped by a series of intentions, entailed by a Social Manifesto, created and located in its own tab of the network website, available for all users. The manifesto is made up of five principles that guide all media deployed on the site. 
Here are the tenets:
1. Strive for Transparency & Accessibility

2. Continually look for the gaps & silences in the scholarship we share

3. Welcome discourse between the user and the collection

4. Promote positionality in knowledge production 

The intention behind creating a manifesto is to emphasize our goals in creating an ethical post-modern digital humanities project. We would like to make our intentions transparent to our viewers and allow the audience autonomy to make an informed decision about the knowledge that they are engaging with. Poetics and narrative play a tremendous role in the production of historical knowledge, and acknowledging the nature of truly nuanced scholarship will be invaluable to expanding the public imagination. As Hayden White encompasses in “The Historical Text as Literary Artifact” in Geoffrey Roberts ed., The History and Narrative Reader, “We may only know the actual through contrast with, or likening to, the imaginable.”

The direct intention of UNRAVEL is to provide a space for people to engage with research and scholarship in a way that allows for more intimate interaction with the material. We aim for clarity, support, and innovation for each of our viewers. After engaging with the network, we hope for viewers to come away feeling like they not only have a great understanding of the topic and access to resources, but to feel inclined to discussion, to breakthroughs, to widen the web that each topic has opened. We intend for our viewers to gain a better understanding of modern topics while understanding their place in the topic, our place in facilitating knowledge, and the topic’s place in their own lives. Understanding that bias runs rampant and being able to notice it, work with it, and minimize are essential to UNRAVEL. We hope for the network to be one of many spaces for expansive engagement, a model for what accessible, interesting, and reliable knowledge can look like.

## *Episode Medium* (ANANYA)

Video essays, a newly popular form of scholarship, provide much more intimacy and accessibility in engagement through direct portrayal of the authors, digital visualization, and more expansive access to dialogue on the content. For “PSYOPtics,” one of the largest topics at hand is the understanding of the sculpting of public imagination, primarily through misinformation and ambiguity. Throughout our mission, information transparency takes a central role and ambiguity is leveled largely through the interactive, conversational, and recognition-oriented medium of video essay. The accessibility of a video essay provides much more room for engagement with the authors, the topic, and clarity through visualizations of our research on domestic psychological operations in America. Through the Network, the audience will get ample opportunity to participate in the conversation, and through video essay format, they will have our research explained to them firsthand with discussion of our own.

Joshua Citarella, an artist, professor at Rhode Island School of Design, and ‘internet culture writer’ is the founder of the nonprofit digital humanities project, “Do Not Research.” His publishing platform allows viewers to engage with various scholarship, art, and cultural media, publish their own, discuss with one another, and join courses that he has created on select topics. The webpage, hosted through Substack, is a curated selection of public submissions which Joshua Citarella parses through and publishes on the site in individual pages.

Citarella also hosts a digital humanities podcast called “Doomscroll” which is mainly centered around interviewing other academics, political figures, and experts on their various fields of expertise. Throughout the interviews, he dives deeper into discussions of how each topic is constructed and positioned within the modern Western sphere. In the episode of interest, “Trevor Paglen: Mindhacking and Psyops Capitalism,” Citarella interviews Trevor Paglen, author, artist, and geographer whose writing focuses greatly on state secrecy, military symbology, and in this episode, psychological operations. Each of his episodes, posted on youtube, are also housed on his substack webpage and include a short-form explanation of the episode along with images or other relevant media. Throughout the episode, Citarella continuously explains and situates the information that his interviewee posits, and accessibly prompts them to further the discussion through questioning or additional information. The episodes as a whole are extremely interesting in how they allow viewers to engage with the material at hand, hear the perspective of an expert on the topic at hand and facilitated by Joshua Citarella’s questioning and clarifying of the topics, which richly builds critical discussion on the topic, both between themselves and for viewers at home.

In the area of inspiration, we intend to create a space for the public to engage with our scholarship and discuss with one another similarly to “Do Not Research,” where the space for discussion is well organized, accessible, and interesting. Some areas that we would like to build upon are directly related to the lack of transparency of information in each episode. Although they use direct names of cases, there are no documents available to engage with directly. As accessibility is of utmost importance, providing our information in a cohesive and accessible way will be invaluable to the broadcast. ## Digital Methods (ANANYA)

The Network, a static site, generated through Jekyll and hosted through Github, will live as an accessible interactive space for our audience to engage with material and actively participate in facilitating the knowledge that we discuss in each episode. A larger goal of ours is to create a space for transparent education and active involvement in examining and situating historical narratives in the current collective memory. ## *Site Method* (ANANYA)

As mentioned earlier, the site is generated through Jekyll, a static site generator with integrated support for Github Pages which allows us to deploy our webpage that we edit on Github. In alignment with our intentions of maintaining a tension between our own [nuance] subjectivity, and [accessibility, transparency, reliability, credibility], Jekyll is described as a simple, user-friendly tool which “doesn't try to outsmart users by making bold assumptions, nor does it burden them with needless complexity and configuration. Put simply, Jekyll gets out of your way and allows you to concentrate on what truly matters: your content.” Github, a cloud-based platform, allows us to edit, track, and collaborate on each facet of the project, as well as house our content which will continuously be updated to the webpage. In building the website, we have used Jekyll and RubyGems, the package of jekyll code language, through Terminal, Mac OS’ command line interface to code and customize the webpage. With regard to troubleshooting common issues and customizing, we have relied on assorted user posts on Reddit as well as Microsoft Copilot and Github’s assorted instructional repositories. With the aim to use the least amount of artificial intelligence possible, for sustainability and ethics reasons, maintaining transparency about and AI involvement is essential to our process.

## *The Age of the Internet: 2010-Present* (ANANYA)

“Automated Text Analysis for Intelligence Purposes: A Psychological Operations Case Study,” a chapter of Open Source Intelligence and Cyber Crime by Bryneilsson et al., examines how modern data-driven methods of analysis alter and progress the process of analysis within “intelligence tasks,” or more specifically, Psychological Operations. We intend to use this source to examine the development of psychological operations into the age of the internet. With relatively newfound accessibility to text analysis tools for data mining, the vast increase in data size, assisted by data mining opens doors for previously unheard of capabilities for fast tracking and disguising intelligence operations. Although this case study addresses foreign operations, it heavily informs the progression of psychological operations domestically as well. The book is housed on Springer Nature Link, a research publishing platform for academic scholarship worldwide.

“On influence operations: brainpower as a weapon of choice” by G.L. Lamborn, a retired CIA agent and Army reserve officer, is an undoubtedly interesting take on the state of modern PSYOPs. Although Lamborn has written a credibly sourced article, his position as a former CIA agent is unlike the variety of dominant views. He depicts a complex disappointment with the state of Psychological operations, sharing his opinions about the necessity to rebuild the former "brainpower" that the US had possessed during influence operations. He calls for an increase in PSYOP funding, a reemphasis on its integration into war planning, and reengaged to the frontlines. We plan to use this source in our comparative analysis to address the modern perspectives on Psychological Operations from a non-civillian standpoint as it has developed in the digital age, Lamborn’s take being especially interesting as a pro-PSYOP position.
